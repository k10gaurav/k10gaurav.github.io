Consultative Selling – WSO2 IAM (Scenario-Based Q&A)

Scenario 1
Category: Discovery & Compliance
A bank says:
“We want to implement WSO2 Identity Server because our regulator is asking for stronger authentication.”

What is the best consultative response to move the conversation forward?
+

Consultative Response

“Can you help me understand which specific regulatory controls are being highlighted—strong customer authentication, auditability, identity lifecycle, or fraud prevention? Also, what risks has the regulator pointed out if these gaps remain?”

Why

This reframes the conversation around regulatory outcomes and risk mitigation instead of jumping straight to a product decision.
Scenario 2
Category: Problem Discovery
A healthcare provider says:
“Our users face too many login issues.”

What is the most consultative way to determine whether IAM is the real problem?
+

Consultative Response

“Do these issues occur during authentication, MFA validation, or application authorization? And are they impacting patients, clinicians, or third-party partners?”

Why

This helps isolate IAM issues from application or infrastructure problems and ties the impact to patient care.
Scenario 3
Category: Identity Scope
A retail enterprise says:
“We want SSO across all our applications.”

What should a consultative seller explore before proposing a solution?
+

Consultative Response

“Which applications are involved, and do they span on-prem, cloud, and partner ecosystems? How are identities provisioned and deprovisioned today?”

Why

SSO without lifecycle governance can introduce security and operational risks.
Scenario 4
Category: Assumption Validation
A government agency insists on deploying IAM on-prem for security reasons.

What consultative response helps validate this requirement?
+

Consultative Response

“Is this requirement driven by formal policy or data residency regulations, or is it based on perceived risk? Are any workloads already hybrid?”

Why

This separates real constraints from assumptions that may limit architectural flexibility.
Scenario 5
Category: Business Impact
A customer says:
“Onboarding new applications into IAM takes months.”

What should be explored next?
+

Consultative Response

“What business impact does this delay create—missed launches, security exceptions, or increased operational overhead?”

Why

Business impact clarifies urgency and justifies IAM modernization.
Scenario 6
Category: Scalability Diagnosis
A fintech company experiences authentication failures during peak hours.

What should be diagnosed before recommending IAM scaling?
+

Consultative Response

“Let’s review authentication flows, MFA dependencies, token lifetimes, and concurrency behavior during peak traffic.”

Why

Many peak-time failures are caused by design inefficiencies rather than lack of capacity.
Scenario 7
Category: Developer Experience
Developers bypass centralized IAM and implement authentication directly in applications.

How should this be interpreted consultatively?
+

Consultative Response

“What challenges do developers face with the current IAM—complex integrations, slow changes, or lack of guidance?”

Why

This reframes the issue as an adoption and usability problem, not a governance failure.
Scenario 8
Category: User Experience
A customer complains about poor login experience.

What should be analyzed before proposing changes?
+

Consultative Response

“Where do users drop off—during MFA, consent, or federation? Do we have metrics on success rate and login time?”

Why

UX issues must be validated using measurable authentication data.
Scenario 9
Category: Change Motivation
A customer wants to replace their legacy IAM but cannot clearly explain why.

What consultative question helps uncover the real driver?
+

Consultative Response

“If the current IAM remains for the next three years, what concerns you most—compliance, scalability, security, or operational cost?”

Why

This reveals the true motivation behind the change.
Scenario 10
Category: Architecture Flexibility
IAM changes frequently require production downtime.

What should be explored consultatively?
+

Consultative Response

“Which changes cause downtime—policies, authentication logic, or integrations—and how often do these changes occur?”

Why

This links business pain to architectural limitations.
Scenario 11
Category: Solution Framing
A bank needs strong customer authentication, regulatory compliance, and minimal login friction.

How should WSO2 IAM be positioned consultatively?
+

Consultative Response

“We should design authentication that adapts to user risk—strong when required, seamless when possible, and fully auditable.”

Why

This focuses on outcomes instead of features.
Scenario 12
Category: Multi-Tenancy
A B2B SaaS provider needs fast onboarding of enterprise customers with tenant isolation.

What should guide the consultative discussion?
+

Consultative Response

“How important are tenant isolation, branding, and delegated administration to your customers, and how fast must onboarding happen?”

Why

IAM directly impacts revenue onboarding velocity.
Scenario 13
Category: Objection Handling
A customer asks:
“Can WSO2 IAM replace our custom OAuth implementation?”

What is the correct consultative approach?
+

Consultative Response

“Let’s understand why OAuth was built in-house—flexibility, performance, or constraints at that time—before deciding.”

Why

This respects past decisions and avoids defensive selling.
Scenario 14
Category: Future Readiness
IAM is required only for employees today, but B2C use cases are planned later.

How should this influence the recommendation?
+

Consultative Response

“We should ensure today’s design doesn’t limit future scale, external identities, or federation needs.”

Why

This avoids re-architecture later.
Scenario 15
Category: Cost Justification
A customer is concerned about IAM license cost.

What value-based discussion should be initiated?
+

Consultative Response

“How much effort is spent today on identity-related support, audits, incident response, and custom development?”

Why

This reframes cost as total cost of ownership.
Scenario 16
Category: Build vs Buy
A customer says:
“We can build IAM ourselves.”

What is the most consultative response?
+

Consultative Response

“You can—so the key question is whether identity is a differentiator or a shared capability best standardized.”

Why

This highlights opportunity cost without confrontation.
Scenario 17
Category: Stakeholder Alignment
Security teams want strict authentication while business teams want frictionless access.

How should IAM be positioned?
+

Consultative Response

“We can use risk-based authentication so controls adapt to context rather than applying blanket rules.”

Why

IAM becomes a balancing mechanism, not a blocker.
Scenario 18
Category: PoC Risk
A customer wants a quick PoC but avoids discovery workshops.

What risk does this pose?
+

Consultative Response

“A PoC may prove technology works but miss the real business problem, leading to rework later.”

Why

Discovery ensures relevance, not just functionality.
Scenario 19
Category: Technical Debt
Post go-live, the customer requests frequent IAM customizations.

What consultative guidance should be given?
+

Consultative Response

“Before customizing, let’s explore configuration, policy modeling, or process changes.”

Why

This avoids long-term maintenance burden.
Scenario 20
Category: Adoption & Change Management
WSO2 IAM is successfully deployed, but adoption is low.

What should be addressed next?
+

Consultative Response

“Is adoption impacted by user experience, awareness, missing integrations, or process resistance?”

Why

IAM success depends on people and process, not just technology.
